Design Approach

We combine a Treatable Traits (TT) framework with Experience-Based Co-Design (EBCD). Findings from our systematic review, cohort study, qualitative interviews, and Delphi consensus are used as trigger materials to prioritise which traits matter most and to co-produce practical tools that fit real PR pathways.

Treatable Traits (TT) in AATD

  • Identify traits: e.g., breathlessness variability, deconditioning, anxiety, oxygen setup, pacing/energy conservation, flare risk.
  • Map traits → actions: assessment cues, behaviour/education components, exercise targets, oxygen checks, pacing/breathing strategies.
  • Track & review: simple indicators (RPE, step-ups/walk distance, daily activity, symptom change) to guide weekly progression.

Experience-Based Co-Design (EBCD)

  1. Trigger evidence: summaries from prior studies + short experience stories.
  2. Co-design workshops: patients and HCPs prioritise needs, critique drafts, and propose changes.
  3. Rapid prototyping: iterate wording, layout, and workflows between sessions.
  4. Usability testing: quick tasks (fill the form, set targets), think-aloud, and short scales.
  5. Refinement: incorporate feedback, prepare beta versions for small-scale use.

What We Are Prototyping

  • Trait→Plan checklist (A4): clinician sheet linking TT → assessment → action → follow-up.
  • Exercise Passport (A6): patient booklet with targets, RPE 0–10, and a progression rule (+5–10%/week when within target and no red flags).
  • Stay-Active Kit: quick-use cards — two-week ramp, good/bad day cues, breathing & pacing, resistance-band basics, habit tracker, relapse/flare plan, oxygen setup checklist.
  • Education for HCPs: concise slides/handouts on AATD-specific PR adaptations, referral pathways, and follow-up planning.

Usability & Acceptability Evaluation

  • Clarity & completeness: can users find the right trait and action quickly?
  • Workload & fit: time to complete; alignment with PR assessment and discharge workflows.
  • Usefulness: perceived value for personalisation, oxygen/pacing decisions, and patient self-management.
  • Feasibility in practice: printing size, durability, where it “lives” (clinic room, patient booklet, EPR upload).
  • Simple metrics: SUS-style items (1–5), task success, and short free-text comments.

Planned Analysis

  • Descriptive stats for ratings (median, IQR) and task timing/completion.
  • Qualitative rapid notes thematic summary (what to keep, change, add).
  • Version log of changes to maintain an audit trail.
